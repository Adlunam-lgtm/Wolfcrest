A note on formulation

Most joint supplements contain MSM. Ours does not.

MSM is inexpensive, widely recognised, and present in the majority of pet joint supplements on the market. The published veterinary research, read carefully, consistently shows very limited evidence for its benefit in dogs. So we left it out. The full reasoning is at wolfcrestnaturals.ca/why-no-msm

What we put in instead

New Zealand Green-Lipped Mussel at 300mg per chew. A 2023 peer-reviewed trial of 75 dogs with hip osteoarthritis found outcomes at 4 and 6 weeks comparable to carprofen, a prescription anti-inflammatory. That is what the same budget buys when it is not spent on MSM.

Every other ingredient in Mobility+ follows the same logic. If it is in the formula, there is a study behind it. If the study has limits, those limits are described on the science page.

The Research

The research behind each ingredient.

Most ingredients have published research behind them. Some have canine-specific clinical trials. Some have strong mechanistic evidence but limited dog-specific data. Two sit closer to long-established veterinary practice than controlled trials. Each card below says which category applies, and links to what actually exists.

Strong clinical evidence
Green-Lipped Mussel
A 2023 peer-reviewed, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of 75 dogs with hip osteoarthritis found that the marine fatty acid compound from New Zealand green-lipped mussel produced outcomes at 4 and 6 weeks comparable to carprofen, a prescription anti-inflammatory.
📄 Kampa et al. (2023), Frontiers in Veterinary Science
Read the study
Strong clinical evidence
L. rhamnosus LGG
Cornell University College of Veterinary Medicine specifically names the LGG strain as effective for managing diarrhea in dogs. It is one of the most consistently cited canine probiotic recommendations in veterinary practice.
🏛 Cornell University College of Veterinary Medicine
Cornell Vet reference
Promising evidence
Glucosamine + Chondroitin
A randomised, double-blind multicentre trial found statistically significant improvements in pain, weight-bearing, and OA severity by day 70. The overall evidence is encouraging but not definitive. More recent trials have shown stronger outcomes for marine-based omega-3 compounds.
📄 McCarthy et al. (2007), The Veterinary Journal
PubMed
Promising evidence
Boswellia Serrata
A randomised, placebo-controlled, double-blind PLOS ONE trial (2022) of 40 dogs with osteoarthritis found significant improvements in pain scores assessed by both veterinarians and owners at 60 days, using a combination formula that included Boswellia serrata.
📄 Comblain et al. (2022), PLOS ONE
Read the study
Promising evidence
B. longum BL999
A blinded, crossover Purina study of 24 anxious Labrador Retrievers found that 90% showed reduced anxiety behaviours, 83% had lower cortisol levels, and 75% had lower heart rates. The full study has not yet appeared in a peer-reviewed journal. The summary and methods available are linked below.
🔬 McGowan et al., Purina Institute
Purina Institute
Well-established mechanism
Turmeric + Bioperine®
Curcumin's NF-kB inhibition mechanism is well documented in published research. Bioperine increases curcumin absorption by up to 20x. Without piperine, most curcumin passes through the gut wall unabsorbed. Both are in the formula.
📄 Henrotin et al. (2010), curcuminoid anti-inflammatory mechanism
PubMed
Well-established mechanism
Vitamin C (Calcium Ascorbate)
Collagen synthesis is biochemically impossible without vitamin C. It is required for the hydroxylation of proline and lysine, two amino acids that form the stable triple-helix structure of collagen. This is established biochemistry, not contested. No canine-specific RCT exists for vitamin C supplementation in isolation, but the biological requirement is not in question.
📄 NIH Office of Dietary Supplements , Vitamin C and collagen biosynthesis
NIH reference
Promising evidence
Hyaluronic Acid (oral)
A prospective, randomised, double-blind clinical study of 55 dogs found that oral hyaluronic acid supplementation for 10 weeks significantly increased HA concentration in synovial fluid and significantly decreased the pro-inflammatory marker paraoxonase-1, compared to placebo. HA concentration declined in the placebo group over the same period.
📄 Leal et al. (2021), Animals , oral HA in dogs post-CCL surgery
PMC full text
Promising evidence
L. acidophilus NCFM
In healthy adult dogs, L. acidophilus supplementation survived gastrointestinal transit, increased fecal lactobacilli, decreased clostridial organisms, and was associated with systemic immune changes. The NCFM strain specifically has over 45 published clinical trials across species, making it the most studied L. acidophilus strain in existence.
📄 Baillon et al. (2004), American Journal of Veterinary Research
PubMed
Strong clinical evidence
E. faecium SF68
Multiple canine-specific controlled trials. A double-blind shelter study of 182 dogs showed lower diarrhea rates in the SF68 group. A separate study in puppies supplemented from weaning to one year showed stimulation of immune function including measurable increases in circulating immunoglobulin A and G. SF68 is one of two E. faecium strains approved as a feed additive for dogs by the European Commission.
📄 Benyacoub et al. (2003), Journal of Nutrition , immune function in young dogs
PubMed
Promising evidence
B. coagulans BC30®
Canine-specific studies exist on stool quality and intestinal health indicators. Cornell University College of Veterinary Medicine has an active funded research award on BC30's effects on amino acid absorption and fecal microbiome in dogs. The strain has over 20 published clinical studies across species and is the only Bacillus strain with FDA GRAS status.
📄 Cornell Vet , BC30 amino absorption and fecal microbiome in dogs
Cornell Vet
Promising evidence
Inulin / FOS (chicory root)
A study from Texas A&M's Gastrointestinal Laboratory evaluated FOS and inulin supplementation directly in healthy dogs and cats over 16 days using 16S rRNA fecal profiling, finding microbiota changes attributable to prebiotic administration. Inulin consistently increases Bifidobacterium across randomised controlled trials in multiple species.
📄 Pinna et al. (2017), PeerJ , fecal microbiota in dogs and cats with FOS/inulin
PubMed
Veterinary practice, limited controlled trials
Organic Pumpkin Powder
Pumpkin is one of the most universally recommended foods by vets for canine digestive issues. Its mechanism is well understood: soluble fibre absorbs excess water in diarrhea; insoluble fibre adds bulk and draws water in constipation. No controlled trial isolates pumpkin powder alone in dogs. It appeared as one component of a fibre formula that significantly improved chronic diarrhea in 31 dogs in a prospective multicenter study.
📄 Forster et al. (2022), BMC Veterinary Research , fibre-supplemented diet in dogs with chronic diarrhea
PMC full text
Veterinary practice, limited controlled trials
Digestive Enzyme Blend
Amylase, protease, and lipase are the enzymes the canine pancreas produces naturally to digest food. Their necessity is not in question. For dogs with exocrine pancreatic insufficiency, enzyme replacement is an evidence-based treatment. For healthy dogs, one controlled study in 14 Beagles found no improvement in macronutrient digestibility with enzyme supplementation. The rationale for inclusion is supportive rather than clinical, particularly for older dogs.
📄 Villaverde et al. (2017), British Journal of Nutrition , enzyme supplements in healthy adult dogs
PubMed
Well-established mechanism
Ginger Root Extract
Ginger is used in veterinary practice as an anti-nausea supplement. VCA Animal Hospitals documents its use for motion sickness and post-chemotherapy nausea in dogs. One study showed dogs receiving a chemotherapeutic agent experienced less vomiting when given ginger. The gingerol and shogaol compounds accelerate gastric motility. No standalone canine RCT on digestive benefit exists in the published literature.
🏛 VCA Animal Hospitals , ginger in veterinary use
VCA reference

Natural Sourcing

Where each ingredient comes from.

Each compound below exists in nature and is recognisable to your dog's body. Most are found in food, in the gut, or produced by the body itself. We list the source because it is part of understanding what you are giving your dog.

🐚
Green-Lipped Mussel
Marlborough Sounds, New Zealand
Sustainably farmed in New Zealand's protected coastal waters. Freeze-dried whole, not extracted, to preserve the full natural complex of omega-3 ETA, glucosamine, chondroitin, and minerals.
🦐
Glucosamine Sulfate
Wild-caught shellfish shells
Derived from the discarded shells of wild-caught shrimp, a natural by-product of the seafood industry. The same glucosamine molecule your dog's cartilage produces. We use the sulfate form, which has better bioavailability than the HCl form found in most veterinary supplements.
🌳
Boswellia Serrata
India, tapped from Boswellia trees
Resin tapped from the bark of Boswellia trees, the same resin used in Ayurvedic medicine for centuries. Standardised to 65% boswellic acids so every dose delivers the same potency.
🌿
Turmeric Extract
India, Curcuma longa root
Ground from turmeric root, a kitchen staple used medicinally for 4,000 years. Concentrated to 95% curcuminoids. Paired with Bioperine so it is actually absorbed rather than passing through.
🦠
Probiotic Strains
Cultured through natural fermentation
Five strains, each naturally found in mammalian intestines. Cultured through fermentation, the oldest form of food preservation in existence. Each strain is named on the label with its CFU count.
🌱
Inulin / FOS
Belgium, chicory root
Extracted from chicory root. Found naturally in garlic, leeks, and bananas. Your dog does not digest it. It travels intact to the colon where it feeds the beneficial bacteria we have introduced through the probiotic strains.
🎃
Pumpkin Powder
Organic, Canadian farms preferred
Organic pumpkin, dried and powdered. 1,000mg per serving. The same vegetable recommended by vets for digestive upsets, at a dose that has a digestive effect rather than a decorative one.
🫚
Ginger Root Extract
India, Zingiber officinale
The same ginger used in cooking and tea for millennia. Standardised to 5% gingerols for a consistent dose. Supports gastric motility and reduces nausea. One of the few digestive ingredients that has both thousands of years of traditional use and a growing body of clinical literature behind it.
🐄
Chondroitin Sulfate
Bovine cartilage
Extracted from the cartilage of cattle, where it serves the same structural role it does in your dog's joints. Chondroitin is the most abundant proteoglycan in articular cartilage, acting as a sponge that draws water into the tissue to keep it cushioned and shock-absorbent.
🍊
Vitamin C (Calcium Ascorbate)
Ascorbic acid, found in all plant foods
Vitamin C in its calcium ascorbate form, chosen over plain ascorbic acid because it is gentler on the stomach. Found in every piece of fruit in existence. In the formula because collagen synthesis is biochemically impossible without it. It is the enzyme that stitches collagen fibres together.
💧
Hyaluronic Acid
Microbial fermentation (rooster comb historically; now fermentation-derived)
Naturally present in the synovial fluid of every joint in your dog's body, where it acts as a lubricant and shock absorber. Production declines with age. The fermentation-derived form used here is the same molecule the body makes, not a synthetic substitute.
🌶️
Black Pepper Extract (Bioperine)
Piperine from Piper nigrum
The compound that gives black pepper its heat, concentrated and standardised. Its only job in the formula is to increase curcumin absorption by up to 20x. Turmeric without piperine has very poor bioavailability. A small amount of one kitchen spice making another one actually work.
